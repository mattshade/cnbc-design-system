Case study

AI platform validation

NBC News Group · Data AI & Emerging Tech — structured evaluation of agentic platforms to decide where we should standardize versus experiment, across both technical builders and newsroom “citizen” users of AI.

Evaluation Agents Governance

Situation & context

Agent frameworks, managed runtimes, and “bring your own stack” OSS options were all viable—but not interchangeable under our security, data, and operational constraints. Platform engineering, product, and architecture partners needed a single, repeatable way to compare candidates without every team running a one-off bake-off.

The audience split mattered: builders (SDKs, observability, long-running agents) versus citizen users (guided experiences, guardrails, support model). Any recommendation had to speak to both—plus a path to defend spend and roadmap to leadership.

What we set out to do

Agent frameworks and managed runtimes were moving faster than any single team could adopt blindly. I worked with engineering, product, and platform partners to validate competing agentic technologies against NBC News Group needs: security and compliance, observability, developer experience, memory and retrieval patterns, cost trajectory, and fit for technical users (building and operating agents) versus citizen users (consuming guided experiences with guardrails).

The goal was not a single “winner” in the abstract—it was a priority stack we could defend to leadership: what to invest in first, what to pilot on the side, and what to revisit when the market matured.

How we evaluated platforms

We anchored discussions in an eight-dimension enterprise framework—covering product experience, AI capabilities, analytics, governance, infrastructure, security, and commercial terms—so we weren’t scoring vendors only on “model playground” features. The list below is the full lens; in working sessions we still had to make trade-offs explicit and measurable.

  1. User interface & configurability Accessibility for technical and non-technical users; visual builders versus robust SDKs; templates and time-to-value for common agent patterns.
  2. Communication capabilities Multimodal I/O; multilingual coverage; omnichannel context; APIs and SDKs for embedding agents in real products.
  3. AI model capabilities Model selection and cost trade-offs; fine-tuning; RAG and enterprise knowledge connectivity; human feedback loops for improvement.
  4. Analytics & performance management Usage and operational analytics; business-impact signals where possible; experimentation (e.g. A/B); conversation intelligence for iteration.
  5. Governance & management controls RBAC aligned to org structure; prompt/agent versioning and approval workflows; segregated environments; compliance monitoring.
  6. Infrastructure & reliability Hosting flexibility (public, private, hybrid, on-prem where required); performance and scale; uptime, redundancy, DR; observability and incident readiness.
  7. Security, privacy & enterprise readiness Enterprise partnership and support depth; security and regulatory posture; data handling and privacy controls.
  8. Pricing model Transparent, scalable commercial terms aligned to usage and growth—so forecasts didn’t fall apart after the first pilot.

Stack comparison lens

For comparing managed stacks versus OSS paths, we distilled that framework into five scoring dimensions—enough to drive debate without a hundred-row spreadsheet:

  • Enterprise fit: IAM, encryption, audit trails, logging/monitoring, and operational maturity.
  • Agent runtime: Long-running tasks, tool use, failure modes, and cost when usage spikes.
  • Memory & RAG: Session vs persistent memory, knowledge bases, and integration with our data posture.
  • Developer experience: SDK quality, portability, multi-agent patterns, and time-to-first reliable agent.
  • Lock-in vs flexibility: Managed convenience versus the ability to move models or clouds later.

Validation lifecycle

Platform selection was one thread; agentic systems also need proof under realistic load, human oversight, and guardrails. The phases below mirror how we structured validation beyond vendor demos, blending ML-style metrics, system stress tests, safety checks, and continuous oversight.

  1. Pre-validation setup Define outcomes and constraints up front; pick KPIs tied to user or operational impact (not only accuracy); map scenarios including edge and adversarial cases.
  2. Offline testing Sandbox runs; long-horizon multi-step workflows; stress with noisy or incomplete inputs; bias and fairness checks where outputs are user-facing; sanity on explainability where vendors surface traces.
  3. Human-in-the-loop review Domain and end-user perspectives on trust and usefulness; explicit escalation and override paths; approvals for high-stakes actions until reliability is demonstrated.
  4. Guardrails & safety validation Policy and role boundaries; data-access limits; red-team and misuse probes; confirm fallbacks (stop, rollback, human handoff) behave as advertised.
  5. Pilot & rollout Controlled pilots; A/B or baseline comparisons where feasible; telemetry on actions, failures, and overrides; feedback loops for iteration.
  6. Ongoing validation Audits and drift monitoring; alerts for anomalies and safety triggers; documentation and system cards that stay current as behavior changes.

Agent-specific emphasis: end-to-end task completion versus brittle demos; autonomy and escalation rates; whether “reasoning” artifacts are reviewable; and whether safety harnesses fire under anomalies—not only whether the model answers a prompt.

Process & deliverables

What we produced

  • A single scoring model (weights + evidence prompts) so different teams didn’t invent incompatible scorecards.
  • Architecture-ready notes: IAM, data residency, logging, and failure-mode expectations per stack.
  • Leadership-facing readouts: trade-off narrative, not feature matrices—enough to justify sequencing and pilot budget.
  • A pilot backlog: thin vertical slices that would actually stress memory, tools, and cost—not “hello world” demos.
  • Validation transparency: a written record of methods, scenarios, and gaps—plus model/system-card style summaries for higher-risk pilots.

Outcomes

  • Aligned vocabulary across product, platform, and newsroom stakeholders on what “agentic” meant in our context.
  • Clear tiers: primary investment path, complementary experiments, and “revisit in 12–18 months” candidates.
  • Less thrash: fewer one-off spikes and more reuse of patterns (observability, guardrails, eval hooks).
  • Citizen + builder guardrails called out explicitly so internal tools didn’t optimize for only one audience.

Outcome

The work produced a shared vocabulary and a clearer prioritization for NBC News Group: which platforms matched our enterprise bar first, which served as strategic complements, and which were better parked for a later revisit—so engineering could move from debate to repeatable delivery on agents without betting the org on hype.

Practically, teams could point to named criteria when pushing back on a vendor or green-lighting a pilot—reducing “favorite framework” debates and making spend and risk legible to people who would never read a SDK diff.
